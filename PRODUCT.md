# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Technical hiring managers, ML/AI engineers, creative technologists, and potential collaborators encountering Paolo for the first time. They arrive evaluating, not browsing: is this person worth talking to? Their context ranges from a recruiter tab-switching during a lunch break to a senior engineer doing genuine due diligence. The site has seconds to make the case.

## Product Purpose

Personal brand platform for Paolo Sandejas, AI/ML engineer and creative technologist. The site's job is to build a recognizable, distinctive presence in the AI/ML and creative tech space — not just to list credentials, but to make Paolo's specific combination of technical depth and creative sensibility legible and memorable. Success is not "impressive." Success is "I remember this person" and "I want to reach out."

## Brand Personality

Creative, precise, human.

Voice: technically fluent and direct, without jargon for its own sake. Tone: confident without being boastful, playful without being performative. Emotional goal: leave visitors with the sense that this person is unusual in the best way — someone who thinks carefully and builds things that feel considered.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS landing pages:** Inter font, gradient CTA buttons, hero-metric templates, testimonial carousels. The startup-kit aesthetic implies commodity; this site must not.
- **Dark mode "hacker" portfolios:** Neon on black, terminal rain, edgy typography. Trying too hard to signal technical identity becomes its own kind of generic.
- **Blank white minimalism:** A centered paragraph on white space reads as lack of confidence or lack of thought. This brand has a point of view; the design must express it.
- **AI-generated slop aesthetics:** Glassmorphism, gradient text, identical card grids, hero-metric layouts. The worst irony for an AI/ML engineer's portfolio would be a design that looks AI-generated.

## Design Principles

1. **Tactility earns credibility.** The riso-print aesthetic — grain, chunky shadows, postcard metaphors, dashed borders — makes technical work feel physical and considered. Every surface should feel like it could be printed. If an element could exist on a screen but not on paper, question whether it belongs.

2. **Show, don't describe.** The site argues for Paolo's creative-technical combination by embodying it, not by stating it. The design is the proof. ASCII art in a project card is more convincing than the word "creative" in a bio.

3. **Be specific, not impressive.** Avoid decoration that exists to signal effort. Every visual element must do work: communicate a fact, establish hierarchy, or build personality. Anything that's only there to look good should be cut.

4. **Precision over polish.** JetBrains Mono, 1.5px borders, exact 4px grid increments — the aesthetic is deliberate, not smooth. Polish implies hiding the seams; precision means the seams are part of the design.

5. **Earn the unconventional.** The bold 4-color palette and riso-print identity work because they are internally consistent and intentional. New elements must prove they belong to the same system — not just be interesting in isolation.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA compliance. Strong color contrast between ink (#1A130A) and paper (#F2EBDA) as the base layer. Reduced-motion alternatives for all Framer Motion animations — the animations reinforce personality but must not exclude users with vestibular sensitivities. Keyboard navigation support throughout.
